This analysis examines the energy footprint of US data centers, with a specific focus on separating cryptocurrency mining operations from AI/hyperscale facilities. This distinction is critical because these two categories have fundamentally different energy consumption patterns:

  • Crypto mining runs at 95% utilization 24/7 (continuous computation)
  • AI data centers run at ~70% utilization (bursty training workloads)

Key Finding

US data centers consume approximately 112.3 TWh annually, representing ~2.8% of total US electricity. Crypto mining (36.9 TWh) and AI/hyperscale operations (43.2 TWh) together account for 71% of this consumption despite representing only 11% of facilities by count.

3. Methodology

3.1 Data Pipeline Overview

4,592 scraped data centers
↓ Filter 1: Remove planned/announced facilities (-626)
↓ Filter 2: Require operational year (-3,285)
↓ Filter 3: Exclude future (year > 2026) (-40)
↓ Filter 4: Remove duplicates (-19)
621 operational data centers with validated data
↓ Classify by keywords + capacity thresholds
↓ Apply physics-based energy formula
Output: Energy estimates by category and state

3.2 Why We Filter to 621 Data Centers

The strict filtering is necessary for accurate energy modeling:

Data Quality Challenge

87% of data centers in our dataset lack an operational year. Without knowing when a facility opened, we cannot:

  • Determine if it's currently operational
  • Track energy growth over time
  • Correlate with EIA electricity data

We include all 4,592 in the map for completeness but use only the 621 validated DCs for energy calculations.

4. Data Center Classification

4.1 Classification Algorithm

Each data center is classified using a hierarchical keyword + capacity approach:

def classify_datacenter(name, capacity):
# Step 1: Check for crypto keywords FIRST
if any(kw in name for kw in CRYPTO_KEYWORDS):
return 'crypto'

# Step 2: Check for AI/hyperscaler keywords
if any(kw in name for kw in AI_KEYWORDS):
return 'big_ai'

# Step 3: Classify by capacity
if capacity >= 50: return 'big_ai'
if capacity >= 10: return 'decent'
return 'small'

5. Energy Estimation Model

5.1 Physics-Based Formula

Energy consumption is calculated using the standard data center energy formula:

Energy (MWh) = Capacity (MW) × 8,760 hours × Utilization × PUE

Where:

  • Capacity: IT power load in megawatts
  • 8,760: Hours per year
  • Utilization: Average % of capacity used
  • PUE: Power Usage Effectiveness (total power / IT power)

5.2 Category Parameters

Category Utilization PUE Default MW MWh per MW Rationale
Crypto 95% 1.10 50 MW 9,161 ASICs run 24/7 at max, minimal cooling overhead
AI/Hyperscale 70% 1.15 50 MW 7,056 Bursty training jobs, efficient cooling
Medium 60% 1.40 15 MW 7,358 Colocation, mixed workloads
Small 50% 1.60 2 MW 7,008 Enterprise, less efficient cooling

5.3 Example Calculations

Riot Platforms Corsicana (Crypto Mining)

Capacity: 1,000 MW | Utilization: 95% | PUE: 1.10

Energy = 1,000 × 8,760 × 0.95 × 1.10 = 9,161,400 MWh/year = 9.16 TWh

This single facility uses more electricity than the entire state of Wyoming.

Meta Kansas City (AI/Hyperscale)

Capacity: 50 MW | Utilization: 70% | PUE: 1.15

Energy = 50 × 8,760 × 0.70 × 1.15 = 352,800 MWh/year = 0.35 TWh

7.2 Why Our Estimate is Conservative

Our 112.3 TWh is lower than industry estimates of 160-200 TWh because:

  1. Only 621 of 4,592 DCs included — 87% lack operational year data
  2. Unknown facilities assigned conservative defaults — 2 MW for small, 15 MW for medium
  3. Enterprise data centers underrepresented — Many private DCs not in datacentermap.com

Validation Passed

Despite being conservative overall, our crypto (36.9 TWh) and AI (43.2 TWh) estimates fall within industry benchmark ranges, validating our classification and physics model.

Weak Predictive Signal

The low R² (0.12) indicates that data center additions explain only ~12% of variance in state electricity changes. This is expected because:

  • Data centers are only 2-5% of state electricity
  • Many other factors drive state consumption (weather, population, industry)
  • Our sample size is limited (621 DCs over 23 years)

9. Limitations & Data Quality

9.1 Data Completeness Issues

Issue Impact Mitigation
87% missing operational year Cannot track historical growth Use strict filtering, show all DCs in map
67% missing capacity Must estimate MW values Conservative category-based defaults
Enterprise DCs underreported Total count is underestimate Note in methodology
Planned vs operational unclear May include non-operational Keyword filtering for "planned"

9.2 Model Assumptions

  • Fixed PUE by category — Real PUE varies by climate, design, age
  • Fixed utilization — Actual utilization varies seasonally and by workload
  • Keyword classification — Some facilities may be misclassified
  • Linear scaling — Energy scales linearly with capacity (simplification)

9.3 What Would Improve the Model

  1. More operational year data — Would allow historical trend analysis
  2. Actual utility bills — Direct measurement vs estimation
  3. Workload data — Training vs inference, batch vs real-time
  4. Cooling technology data — Air, liquid, immersion cooling affects PUE

10. Conclusions

10.1 Summary of Findings

  1. US data centers consume ~112+ TWh annually, approximately 2.8% of total US electricity (conservative estimate)
  2. Crypto mining (36.9 TWh) and AI (43.2 TWh) account for 71% of data center energy despite being only 11% of facilities
  3. Texas dominates with 34.5% of total DC energy consumption
  4. Physics-based estimation provides more reliable results than ML correlation for this problem
  5. Separating crypto from AI is critical for accurate energy modeling

10.2 Policy Implications

  • A single large crypto mining facility can consume more electricity than a small state
  • AI data center growth should be tracked separately from crypto mining
  • State-level grid planning must account for DC clustering (Texas, Virginia)
  • PUE improvements have diminishing returns — utilization patterns matter more

10.3 Future Work

  • Incorporate utility interconnection data for better capacity estimates
  • Track announced projects to forecast future growth
  • Model water usage (WUE) alongside energy
  • Analyze renewable energy procurement by facility

Final Note

This analysis demonstrates that while ML models struggle to isolate data center signals from noisy state-level electricity data, physics-based approaches combined with careful categorization can produce industry-validated energy estimates. The key innovation is separating crypto mining from AI workloads, which have fundamentally different energy profiles.
