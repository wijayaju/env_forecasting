Facilities vs Energy Share

This analysis examines the energy footprint of US data centers, with a specific focus on separating cryptocurrency mining operations from AI/hyperscale facilities. This distinction is critical because these two categories have fundamentally different energy consumption patterns:

  • Crypto mining runs at 95% utilization 24/7 (continuous computation)
  • AI data centers run at ~70% utilization (bursty training workloads)

Key Finding

US data centers consume approximately 112.3 TWh annually, representing ~2.8% of total US electricity. Crypto mining (36.9 TWh) and AI/hyperscale operations (43.2 TWh) together account for 71% of this consumption despite representing only 11% of facilities by count.

AI Prediction Model: Machine learning achieves R² = 0.978 accuracy in predicting state electricity, with population (90%) and GDP (7%) as primary drivers. DC features contribute ~3% — validating the physics estimate that DCs are 3-5% of US electricity.

3. Methodology

3.1 Approach Selection: Physics-Based vs Machine Learning

We evaluated two approaches for estimating data center energy consumption:

✓ Physics-Based Model (Selected)

  • Uses established engineering formulas
  • Parameters grounded in industry standards
  • Transparent and explainable calculations
  • Works well with limited training data
  • Validated against EIA/IEA benchmarks

✗ Pure ML Model (Rejected)

  • Weak signal: DCs are only 2-5% of state electricity
  • Confounding factors: weather, population, industry
  • Limited labeled training data (621 validated DCs)
  • R² = 0.12 indicates poor predictive power
  • Black box — hard to validate assumptions

Key Insight: The ML model's weak performance is actually informative — it tells us that data center energy signals are buried in noise at the state level. This justifies our physics-based approach where we calculate energy from first principles rather than inferring it from consumption data.

  • Track energy growth over time
  • Correlate with EIA electricity data by year

We include all 4,592 in the map for completeness but use only the 621 validated DCs for energy calculations.

3.6 Handling Missing Capacity Data

When a data center's power capacity (MW) is not available, we apply category-based defaults:

Category Default Capacity Justification
Crypto Mining 50 MW Most crypto facilities are purpose-built at scale; smaller operations are rarely publicly listed
AI/Hyperscale 50 MW Hyperscalers build at minimum 50 MW per campus; many exceed 100 MW
Medium (colocation) 15 MW Industry average for Tier III colocation facilities (Uptime Institute data)
Small (enterprise) 2 MW Typical for single-tenant enterprise data centers and edge facilities

Conservative Estimation Strategy

Our default capacities are intentionally conservative to avoid overestimation. For example, while some hyperscale facilities exceed 500 MW, we use 50 MW as the default because:

  • Facilities with known massive capacity are already captured in the dataset
  • Unknown facilities are more likely to be smaller operations
  • This approach yields estimates that align with industry benchmarks

5. Energy Estimation Model

5.1 Physics-Based Formula

Energy consumption is calculated using the standard data center energy formula, derived from fundamental power engineering principles:

Energy (MWh) = Capacity (MW) × 8,760 hours × Utilization × PUE

Formula Breakdown

Variable Unit Description How We Determine It
Capacity MW Maximum IT equipment power load the facility can support From scraped data, or category-based default if unknown
8,760 hours/year Total hours in a year (24 × 365) Fixed constant — DCs operate continuously
Utilization % (0-1) Average percentage of capacity actually used Category-based assumption (see Section 3.7)
PUE ratio (≥1.0) Power Usage Effectiveness = Total Power / IT Power Category-based assumption from industry benchmarks

5.2 Understanding PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness)

PUE is the standard industry metric for data center efficiency, defined by The Green Grid in 2007:

PUE = Total Facility Power / IT Equipment Power

What PUE includes:

  • Numerator (Total Power): Everything consumed by the facility — servers, cooling, lighting, security, UPS losses, power distribution
  • Denominator (IT Power): Only the power consumed by computing equipment (servers, storage, networking)

PUE Examples

  • PUE = 1.0: Perfect efficiency (impossible in practice) — all power goes to computing
  • PUE = 1.1: Hyperscale efficiency — 10% overhead for cooling/infrastructure
  • PUE = 1.5: Average commercial DC — 50% overhead
  • PUE = 2.0: Older/inefficient DC — as much power for cooling as for computing

5.3 Why Crypto Mining Has Lower PUE

Cryptocurrency mining operations achieve significantly better PUE (1.10) than traditional data centers for several reasons:

Factor Traditional DC Crypto Mining
Equipment Type Mixed servers, varied cooling needs Uniform ASICs, optimized heat dissipation
Redundancy N+1 or 2N power/cooling for uptime Minimal redundancy — downtime = lost revenue, not SLA breach
Location Flexibility Near customers, urban areas Remote locations with cheap power, ambient cooling
Cooling Method Precision air conditioning (CRAC units) Air cooling, evaporative, or immersion cooling
Operating Temp Strict 18-27°C range for servers ASICs tolerate higher temps (up to 85°C)

5.4 Why Utilization Varies by Category

Crypto: 95% Utilization

Rationale: Bitcoin mining is a continuous race. Every second an ASIC isn't running, you're losing potential block rewards. Mining operations run at maximum capacity 24/7.

The only downtime: Hardware maintenance (~3-5%), power outages, or deliberate curtailment during grid stress (some miners have demand response contracts).

AI/Hyperscale: 70% Utilization

Rationale: AI workloads are bursty — model training runs at 100% for days/weeks, then inference scales with demand. Hyperscalers also maintain reserve capacity for traffic spikes.

Evidence: Google reports 60-80% average utilization. Microsoft Azure targets 65-75%. Training clusters may hit 95% during active jobs but are idle between runs.

Colocation: 60% Utilization

Rationale: Mixed customer workloads with different peak times. Customers often over-provision capacity for future growth. Contractual power (what customers pay for) exceeds actual usage.

Evidence: Uptime Institute 2023 survey shows colocation facilities average 55-65% utilization of contracted power.

Enterprise: 50% Utilization

Rationale: Enterprise data centers are often sized for peak load (year-end processing, Black Friday) plus growth headroom. Day-to-day utilization is much lower.

Evidence: McKinsey studies show enterprise DCs average 40-60%, with many below 30%. Virtualization has improved this but not eliminated over-provisioning.

5.8 Aggregation to State and National Totals

After calculating energy for each data center, we aggregate:

# Per data center
for dc in datacenters:
dc.energy = dc.capacity × 8760 × dc.utilization × dc.pue

# Per state
state_energy = sum(dc.energy for dc in state.datacenters)
state_crypto = sum(dc.energy for dc in state.datacenters if dc.category == 'crypto')
state_ai = sum(dc.energy for dc in state.datacenters if dc.category == 'big_ai')

# National totals
national_energy = sum(state_energy for state in states)
national_crypto = sum(state_crypto for state in states)
national_ai = sum(state_ai for state in states)

9.2 Model Assumptions

  • Fixed PUE by category — Real PUE varies by climate, design, age
  • Fixed utilization — Actual utilization varies seasonally and by workload
  • Keyword classification — Some facilities may be misclassified
  • Linear scaling — Energy scales linearly with capacity (simplification)

9.3 What Would Improve the Model

  1. More operational year data — Would allow historical trend analysis
  2. Actual utility bills — Direct measurement vs estimation
  3. Workload data — Training vs inference, batch vs real-time
  4. Cooling technology data — Air, liquid, immersion cooling affects PUE

10. Conclusions

10.1 Summary of Findings

  1. US data centers consume ~112+ TWh annually, approximately 2.8% of total US electricity (conservative estimate)
  2. Crypto mining (36.9 TWh) and AI (43.2 TWh) account for 71% of data center energy despite being only 11% of facilities
  3. Texas dominates with 34.5% of total DC energy consumption
  4. Physics-based estimation provides more reliable results than ML correlation for this problem
  5. Separating crypto from AI is critical for accurate energy modeling

10.2 Policy Implications

  • A single large crypto mining facility can consume more electricity than a small state
  • AI data center growth should be tracked separately from crypto mining
  • State-level grid planning must account for DC clustering (Texas, Virginia)
  • PUE improvements have diminishing returns — utilization patterns matter more

10.3 Future Work

  • Incorporate utility interconnection data for better capacity estimates
  • Track announced projects to forecast future growth
  • Model water usage (WUE) alongside energy
  • Analyze renewable energy procurement by facility

Final Note

This analysis demonstrates that while ML models struggle to isolate data center signals from noisy state-level electricity data, physics-based approaches combined with careful categorization can produce industry-validated energy estimates. The key innovation is separating crypto mining from AI workloads, which have fundamentally different energy profiles.
